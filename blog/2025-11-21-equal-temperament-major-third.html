Why the Equal Temperament Major Third Feels "Bent"

This article was compiled by ChatGPT.

Let's dive into it with some geometric analogies that illuminate why the equal temperament major third, about 400 cents, feels the most "bent" or "distorted" compared to its pure just-intonation version, about 386.3 cents with the ratio 5:4.

1. The Circle Division Analogy

Imagine tuning as dividing a circle, the octave, into 12 equal slices. Each semitone is 30 degrees. A pure major third should correspond to the ratio 5:4, which is about 4.86 semitones, or roughly 145.8 degrees on our circle. But equal temperament forces it to land exactly at 4 semitones, 400 cents, or 144 degrees on our circle.

That is like trying to make a perfect pentagon by dividing a circle into 12 equal parts. The points almost line up, but not quite. The geometry is regular, but the proportions are off. The major third is the point where the grid of equal spacing and the natural ratios of harmonic geometry diverge most visibly.

2. The Triangle Stretch Analogy

A pure major third, 5:4, is like an isosceles triangle with sides that meet perfectly to form a harmonious shape. When you temper it, 400 cents instead of 386 cents, it is as if you stretch one side slightly, making the triangle no longer close perfectly. You still have a triangle, but the symmetry is broken.

Intervals like the perfect fifth, 700 cents in equal temperament versus about 702 cents pure, only stretch a tiny bit, so the triangle still looks good. But the major third's deviation is large enough that the figure looks noticeably warped.

3. The Spiral Misalignment Analogy

Think of stacking intervals as tracing a spiral staircase upward. Pure intervals, in just intonation, spiral smoothly so that every few steps land neatly above a harmonic floor. In equal temperament, each step is forced to be the same size, but that makes the spiral twist slightly off-center.

The major third happens where the spiral's mismatch is most visible: the step lands furthest away from the pure harmonic level. That is why chords with equal-tempered major thirds, like C-E-G, have that slight shimmer or beating. The geometry of the frequencies does not quite align.

4. The Grid Distortion Analogy

Imagine drawing a harmonic lattice on graph paper. The horizontal axis is fifths, ratio 3:2. The vertical axis is thirds, ratio 5:4. In just intonation, the grid lines are rationally related. Intersections are clean, forming a rectangular lattice.

Equal temperament slightly shears the grid. Each cell becomes a parallelogram, not a rectangle. The major third line is the one most obviously tilted, because the adjustment needed to make 12 equal semitones fit in an octave causes that line to shift the most.

5. The Harmonic Projection Analogy

If you picture harmonics as points on a smooth curve, the fifth harmonic, which defines the pure major third, lies slightly off the curve defined by equal temperament's 12 equal points. When equal temperament projects that curve onto its uniform lattice, the fifth harmonic point gets displaced the most. That is why the major third becomes the most audibly off.
